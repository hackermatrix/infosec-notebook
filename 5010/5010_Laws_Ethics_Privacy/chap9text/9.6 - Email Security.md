# 9.6 — Email Security

> **Core Idea:** Email is one of the most widely used — and most vulnerable — communication channels. It was designed without security or privacy in mind. Understanding how email works, how it can be intercepted, and how to protect it is essential.

---

## 📬 Where Does Email Go, and Who Can Access It?

### The Path of an Email

```
Sender → Sender's Mail Client → Sender's Mail Server (SMTP)
    → Internet (multiple hops/routers) →
Recipient's Mail Server → Recipient's Mail Client (POP3/IMAP)
```

### Who Can Potentially Access Your Email?

| Entity | How They Access | What They See |
|--------|----------------|---------------|
| **Your email provider** | Stores your email on their servers | Full content of all emails (unless end-to-end encrypted) |
| **Recipient's email provider** | Stores the delivered copy | Full content |
| **Network administrators** | Monitor traffic at your workplace or school | Email content (if unencrypted), metadata |
| **ISPs** | Route your traffic | Metadata; content if unencrypted |
| **Governments** | Legal orders, warrants, NSLs, mass surveillance programs | Content and metadata (varies by jurisdiction and legal authority) |
| **Hackers** | Compromised accounts, server breaches, man-in-the-middle attacks | Whatever they can access |
| **Employer** | Company email is company property | Full content of work email; legally permitted in most jurisdictions |

### Key Reality
> Email is like a **postcard, not a sealed letter**. Without encryption, anyone along the delivery path can potentially read it.

---

## 🕵️ Interception of Email

### How Email Can Be Intercepted

| Method | Description | Defense |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Network sniffing** | Capturing unencrypted email packets on a network | TLS/SSL encryption between servers |
| **Man-in-the-middle** | Intercepting communication between mail servers | Certificate validation, DKIM, SPF |
| **Server compromise** | Hacking into an email server | Server security, access controls |
| **Account compromise** | Stealing login credentials (phishing, password reuse) | Strong passwords, MFA |
| **Physical access** | Accessing an unlocked device with email open | Device encryption, screen locks |
| **Backup interception** | Accessing email from backup tapes or cloud backups | Encrypted backups |

### Email Encryption Solutions

| Solution | Type | Key Feature |
|----------|------|-------------|
| **TLS (Transport Layer Security)** | In-transit encryption | Encrypts email between servers; standard for most providers today; does NOT encrypt at rest |
| **S/MIME** | End-to-end | Uses digital certificates; built into many email clients; requires PKI infrastructure |
| **PGP / GPG** | End-to-end | Uses public-key cryptography; no central authority needed; popular in technical communities |
| **ProtonMail / Tutanota** | End-to-end (built-in) | Encrypted email providers; easy to use; encryption between users of the same service |

#### End-to-End Encryption vs. Transport Encryption
| Aspect | Transport (TLS) | End-to-End (PGP/S-MIME) |
|--------|-----------------|------------------------|
| **What's protected** | Email in transit between servers | Email content from sender to recipient |
| **Email at rest** | NOT encrypted on servers | Encrypted on servers |
| **Provider can read** | Yes | No |
| **Government subpoena** | Provider can comply (has plaintext) | Provider cannot provide plaintext |
| **Ease of use** | Automatic, transparent | Requires key management |

## 👁️ Monitoring Email

### Who Monitors Email and Why?

#### Employers
- **Legally permitted** in most jurisdictions — company email is company property
- Monitor for **policy compliance**, data leakage prevention, productivity, legal liability
- Employees generally have **no expectation of privacy** on work email
- Monitoring tools scan for keywords, attachments, sensitive data patterns

#### Governments
- **Law enforcement** — with warrants or court orders
- **Intelligence agencies** — broader authority under FISA, PATRIOT Act, etc.
- **Metadata analysis** — who emailed whom, when, how often (even without reading content)
- Metadata alone can reveal **social networks, habits, relationships, and patterns**

#### Email Providers
- **Automated scanning** for spam, malware, and (historically) targeted advertising
- Gmail historically scanned email content for ads (discontinued for consumer Gmail)
- Providers comply with **legal data requests** from governments

## 🎭 Anonymous, Pseudonymous, and Disappearing Email

### Anonymous Email
- Sends email without revealing the sender's identity
- **Remailers** — services that strip identifying information and forward email
  - **Type I (Cypherpunk)** — strips headers and forwards
  - **Type II (Mixmaster)** — adds latency and mixing to prevent traffic analysis
  - **Type III (Mixminion)** — adds reply capability and improved mixing
- **Limitations** — content may still identify you; legal in most places but suspicious

### Pseudonymous Email
- Uses a **consistent pseudonym** rather than real identity
- Allows building a reputation without revealing real identity
- Services like ProtonMail, Tutanota allow signup without personal information
- **Disposable email services** (Guerrilla Mail, Temp-Mail) for one-time use

### Disappearing / Self-Destructing Email
- Messages that **delete themselves** after a set time or after being read
- Offered by services like ProtonMail (expiring messages), Snapchat-like email features
- **Limitations:** recipient can screenshot, copy text, or use screen recording before deletion
- Not truly reliable for preventing information retention

## 🎣 Spoofing and Spamming

### Email Spoofing
**Spoofing** = forging the "From" address so an email appears to come from someone else.

- Email protocols (SMTP) were designed **without authentication** — anyone can claim any sender address
- Used in **phishing attacks** — fake emails appearing to come from banks, employers, trusted contacts
- Used for **fraud, impersonation, and social engineering**

#### Anti-Spoofing Technologies

| Technology | Function |
|-----------|----------|
| **SPF (Sender Policy Framework)** | Specifies which mail servers are authorized to send email for a domain |
| **DKIM (DomainKeys Identified Mail)** | Adds a digital signature to emails, verified against a public key in DNS |
| **DMARC (Domain-based Message Authentication, Reporting & Conformance)** | Builds on SPF and DKIM; tells receiving servers what to do with failed authentication (reject, quarantine, none) |

### Email Spam
**Spam** = unsolicited bulk email, usually commercial or malicious.

#### The Spam Problem
- Consumes bandwidth, storage, and user time
- Carries **malware, phishing links, and scams**
- Estimated to be **45-85% of all email traffic**
- Costs businesses billions annually in lost productivity and security incidents

#### Anti-Spam Measures
| Measure | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Content filters** | Analyze email content for spam characteristics (keywords, patterns, formatting) |
| **Bayesian filtering** | Machine learning that learns from user-marked spam/not-spam |
| **Blacklists (DNSBLs)** | Lists of known spam-sending IP addresses |
| **Greylisting** | Temporarily rejects email from unknown senders; legitimate servers retry, spammers usually don't |
| **Rate limiting** | Limits the number of emails a server can send in a given period |
| **CAN-SPAM Act** | U.S. law requiring opt-out mechanism, accurate headers, physical address (does NOT require opt-in) |
| **SPF/DKIM/DMARC** | Prevent spoofing, which many spam campaigns rely on |

## 📝 Summary of Email Privacy

| Aspect | Status | Recommendation |
|--------|--------|---------------|
| **Default email** | Not encrypted at rest; postcard analogy | Use end-to-end encryption for sensitive messages |
| **Work email** | Employer can read it; no privacy expectation | Use personal email for personal matters |
| **Metadata** | Reveals who, when, how often — even without content | Difficult to hide; Tor + encrypted email for maximum privacy |
| **Spoofing** | Trivially easy without SPF/DKIM/DMARC | Verify unexpected emails; organizations should implement SPF/DKIM/DMARC |
| **Spam** | Persistent and often malicious | Use filters; never click links in suspicious emails |

---

## 💡 Key Takeaways

> [!tip] Remember
> - Email is like a **postcard** — not encrypted by default
> - **TLS** encrypts in transit; **PGP/S-MIME** provide end-to-end encryption
> - **Employers can legally read work email** in most jurisdictions
> - **Email metadata** (who, when, how often) is almost as revealing as content
> - **SPF, DKIM, and DMARC** are the three anti-spoofing technologies
> - **CAN-SPAM** requires opt-out but does NOT require opt-in consent
> - **Anonymous remailers** (Type I, II, III) strip sender identity with increasing sophistication
