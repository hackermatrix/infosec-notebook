# 9.2 — Privacy Principles and Policies

> **Core Idea:** Societies have developed legal frameworks, policies, and principles to protect privacy. The U.S. takes a sector-specific approach with individual laws for healthcare, finance, children, etc., while other nations (especially the EU) favor comprehensive, unified data protection legislation.

---

## ⚖️ Fair Information Practices (FIPs)

The **Fair Information Practices** (also called Fair Information Practice Principles — FIPPs) are the foundational principles underlying most modern privacy laws worldwide.

### The Core Principles

| Principle | Meaning |
|-----------|---------|
| **Notice / Awareness** | Organizations must inform individuals about what data they collect, why, and how it will be used — BEFORE collecting it |
| **Choice / Consent** | Individuals must have the ability to choose whether their data is collected and how it's used (opt-in vs. opt-out) |
| **Access / Participation** | Individuals must be able to view their own data and correct inaccuracies |
| **Integrity / Security** | Organizations must take reasonable steps to ensure data is accurate, complete, and protected from unauthorized access |
| **Enforcement / Redress** | There must be mechanisms to enforce these principles and provide remedies when they're violated |

### Opt-In vs. Opt-Out
| Model | Description | Privacy Strength |
|-------|-------------|-----------------|
| **Opt-In** | Data is NOT collected/shared unless the individual explicitly agrees | Stronger — default is privacy |
| **Opt-Out** | Data IS collected/shared unless the individual explicitly objects | Weaker — default is collection |

> The EU generally requires **opt-in** consent. The U.S. generally relies on **opt-out** models.

## 🇺🇸 U.S. Privacy Laws

The U.S. does **not** have a single comprehensive federal privacy law. Instead, it uses a **sectoral approach** — separate laws for different industries and data types.

### Major U.S. Privacy Legislation

| Law | Year | Scope | Key Provisions |
|-----|------|-------|---------------|
| **Fourth Amendment** | 1791 | Government | Protects against unreasonable searches and seizures; requires warrants |
| **Privacy Act** | 1974 | Federal government | Governs how federal agencies collect, maintain, and use personal records; gives citizens access/correction rights |
| **FERPA** (Family Educational Rights and Privacy Act) | 1974 | Education | Protects student education records; gives parents access rights (transfers to student at age 18) |
| **ECPA** (Electronic Communications Privacy Act) | 1986 | Electronic communications | Restricts government wiretapping of electronic communications; includes the Stored Communications Act |
| **HIPAA** (Health Insurance Portability and Accountability Act) | 1996 | Healthcare | Protects health information (PHI); sets standards for data security, breach notification; Privacy Rule and Security Rule |
| **COPPA** (Children's Online Privacy Protection Act) | 1998 | Children under 13 | Requires parental consent before collecting data from children under 13; mandates privacy policies on children's sites |
| **GLBA** (Gramm-Leach-Bliley Act) | 1999 | Financial institutions | Requires financial institutions to explain data-sharing practices; gives consumers opt-out rights; mandates security safeguards |
| **USA PATRIOT Act** | 2001 | National security | Expanded government surveillance powers post-9/11; National Security Letters; FISA Court orders |
| **CAN-SPAM Act** | 2003 | Commercial email | Regulates commercial email; requires opt-out mechanism, accurate headers, physical address; does NOT require opt-in |
| **CCPA / CPRA** (California Consumer Privacy Act / CA Privacy Rights Act) | 2018/2020 | California consumers | Right to know, delete, and opt-out of sale of personal information; applies to businesses meeting certain thresholds; closest U.S. equivalent to GDPR |

### Key Takeaway on U.S. Approach
- **Patchwork** of laws — no single comprehensive federal privacy law
- Different rules for healthcare, finance, education, children, government
- **Gaps** exist — many types of data (general consumer data) have limited federal protection
- **State laws** (especially California's CCPA/CPRA) are filling federal gaps
- Emphasis on **self-regulation** for the commercial sector

## 🏛️ Controls on U.S. Government Websites

Federal agencies must follow specific rules when operating websites:

- **Privacy Impact Assessments (PIAs)** — required before deploying systems that collect PII
- **System of Records Notices (SORNs)** — published in the Federal Register when agencies create or modify record systems
- **Cookie restrictions** — federal websites have limitations on using persistent cookies
- **Privacy policies** — must be prominently posted, written in plain language
- **E-Government Act of 2002** — mandates PIAs for federal IT systems
- **OMB guidance** — Office of Management and Budget issues directives on federal website privacy practices

---

## 🏢 Controls on Commercial Websites

Commercial websites face fewer uniform federal requirements but are subject to:

- **FTC enforcement** — the Federal Trade Commission can pursue companies for deceptive or unfair privacy practices (even without a specific privacy law)
- **Privacy policies** — most states require websites to post them if they collect personal information
- **Children's data** — COPPA applies to commercial sites targeting or knowingly collecting data from children under 13
- **Financial data** — GLBA applies to financial services sites
- **Health data** — HIPAA applies to covered entities and their business associates
- **California (CCPA/CPRA)** — applies to businesses meeting revenue or data thresholds
- **Industry self-regulation** — TRUSTe/TrustArc seals, DAA AdChoices, industry codes of conduct

## 🌍 Non-U.S. Privacy Principles

### EU General Data Protection Regulation (GDPR) — 2018
The GDPR is the world's most comprehensive privacy regulation:

| GDPR Principle | Description |
|----------------|-------------|
| **Lawfulness, Fairness, Transparency** | Processing must have a legal basis and be transparent to data subjects |
| **Purpose Limitation** | Data collected for specified, explicit purposes only |
| **Data Minimization** | Collect only what is necessary for the stated purpose |
| **Accuracy** | Personal data must be kept accurate and up to date |
| **Storage Limitation** | Data kept only as long as necessary |
| **Integrity and Confidentiality** | Appropriate security measures required |
| **Accountability** | Controllers must demonstrate compliance |

#### Key GDPR Rights
- **Right to Access** — see what data is held about you
- **Right to Rectification** — correct inaccurate data
- **Right to Erasure ("Right to Be Forgotten")** — request deletion of personal data
- **Right to Data Portability** — receive your data in a usable format to transfer to another service
- **Right to Object** — object to processing, especially for direct marketing
- **Right Not to Be Subject to Automated Decision-Making** — including profiling

#### GDPR Enforcement
- Fines up to **€20 million or 4% of global annual revenue** (whichever is greater)
- **Data Protection Officers (DPOs)** required for many organizations
- **72-hour breach notification** requirement
- Applies to ANY organization processing EU residents' data, regardless of where the organization is based (**extraterritorial reach**)

### Other International Frameworks
- **OECD Privacy Guidelines (1980)** — foundational principles adopted by many countries
- **APEC Privacy Framework** — Asia-Pacific cooperation framework
- **Canada's PIPEDA** — Personal Information Protection and Electronic Documents Act
- **Brazil's LGPD** — General Data Protection Law (modeled on GDPR)
- **Australia's Privacy Act** — Australian Privacy Principles (APPs)

## 🛡️ Individual Actions to Protect Privacy

Individuals can take steps to protect their own privacy:

- **Read privacy policies** (or at minimum, check data sharing practices) before signing up for services
- **Minimize data sharing** — provide only required information; use pseudonyms where possible
- **Use privacy tools** — VPNs, ad blockers, privacy-focused browsers (Tor, Brave), encrypted messaging
- **Manage cookies** — clear cookies regularly, use browser privacy settings
- **Review permissions** — audit what apps and services have access to your data
- **Opt out** of data broker listings and marketing databases
- **Use strong, unique passwords** and enable multi-factor authentication
- **Be cautious on social media** — limit what you share publicly
- **Monitor financial accounts** and credit reports for signs of identity theft
- **Encrypt** sensitive data and communications

## 🏛️ Governments and Privacy

### The Government Privacy Paradox
Governments have a **dual role**:
1. **Protector of privacy** — enacting and enforcing privacy laws
2. **Threat to privacy** — conducting surveillance for law enforcement and national security

### Government Surveillance Capabilities
- **Mass surveillance programs** — NSA programs revealed by Snowden (PRISM, etc.)
- **National Security Letters (NSLs)** — compel disclosure without judicial oversight
- **FISA Court** — secret court authorizing surveillance of foreign intelligence targets
- **Metadata collection** — phone records, email headers (who contacted whom, when, for how long)
- **Cross-border data requests** — governments demanding data from companies operating in their jurisdiction

### Balancing Act
- **Tension between privacy and security** is ongoing and unresolved
- **Oversight mechanisms** (courts, legislative committees, inspectors general) attempt to prevent abuse
- **Transparency reports** from tech companies disclose government data requests

## 🆔 Identity Theft

### What Is Identity Theft?
Identity theft occurs when someone **uses another person's personal information** (name, SSN, credit card, etc.) without authorization to commit fraud or other crimes.

### Types of Identity Theft

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **Financial identity theft** | Using someone's information to open accounts, make purchases, or obtain credit |
| **Criminal identity theft** | Giving someone else's identity when arrested or charged with a crime |
| **Medical identity theft** | Using someone's identity to obtain medical care or prescriptions |
| **Synthetic identity theft** | Combining real and fabricated information to create a new fake identity |
| **Child identity theft** | Using a child's clean credit history (often goes undetected for years) |
| **Tax identity theft** | Filing fraudulent tax returns using someone's SSN to claim refunds |

### How Identity Theft Happens
- **Data breaches** — massive exposure of PII from compromised databases
- **Phishing** — fraudulent emails/websites that trick people into revealing credentials
- **Social engineering** — manipulating people into disclosing personal information
- **Dumpster diving** — searching through discarded documents containing personal information
- **Mail theft** — stealing physical mail containing financial statements, pre-approved credit offers
- **Skimming** — devices on ATMs or point-of-sale terminals that capture card data
- **Dark web markets** — stolen identities bought and sold

### Protecting Against Identity Theft
- **Monitor credit reports** — free annual reports from each major bureau (Equifax, Experian, TransUnion)
- **Credit freezes** — prevent new accounts from being opened in your name
- **Fraud alerts** — require additional verification before credit is issued
- **Shred documents** containing personal information
- **Strong passwords and MFA** for financial and email accounts
- **Be skeptical** of unsolicited contacts requesting personal information

---

## 💡 Key Takeaways

> [!tip] Remember
> - **Fair Information Practices** (Notice, Choice, Access, Integrity, Enforcement) underpin all privacy law
> - U.S. uses a **sectoral approach** (HIPAA, COPPA, GLBA, etc.) — no single comprehensive law
> - EU's **GDPR** is the gold standard — comprehensive, extraterritorial, steep fines
> - **Opt-in** (EU) vs. **Opt-out** (U.S.) is a fundamental philosophical difference
> - **Identity theft** is a growing consequence of inadequate privacy protection
> - Governments are both **protectors and threats** to privacy
