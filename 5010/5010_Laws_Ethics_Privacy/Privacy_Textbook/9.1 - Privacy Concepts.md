# 9.1 — Privacy Concepts

> **Core Idea:** Privacy is the right of individuals to control how their personal information is collected, used, and shared. In the digital age, computers have created entirely new categories of privacy threats that didn't exist before.

---

## 🔍 Aspects of Information Privacy

### What Is Privacy?

Privacy is **not** a single concept — it has multiple dimensions:

| Aspect | Description | Example |
|--------|-------------|---------|
| **Freedom from intrusion** | Being left alone; not being watched or monitored | Not having your browsing history tracked |
| **Control of information about oneself** | Deciding what personal data is collected and who sees it | Choosing what appears on your social media profile |
| **Freedom from surveillance** | Not being observed, recorded, or tracked without consent | No hidden cameras in private spaces |
| **Protection from unauthorized disclosure** | Keeping sensitive data confidential | Medical records not shared without permission |
| **Anonymity** | Ability to act without being identified | Anonymous voting, anonymous web browsing |
| **Solitude** | Physical and digital space free from disturbance | Not receiving unsolicited marketing calls |

### The Privacy Spectrum

Privacy is **not absolute** — it exists on a spectrum balanced against:
- **Security needs** — law enforcement and national security sometimes require access to private information
- **Public interest** — journalists reporting on public figures, public health emergencies
- **Commercial interests** — businesses want data to provide services and targeted advertising
- **Social norms** — what's considered "private" varies across cultures and generations

### Key Privacy Principles
- **Personally Identifiable Information (PII)** — any data that can identify a specific individual (name, SSN, email, IP address, biometrics)
- **Sensitive data** — a subset of PII requiring extra protection (health records, financial data, sexual orientation, religious beliefs, political affiliation)
- **Data subject** — the individual whose data is being collected/processed
- **Data controller** — the entity that decides how and why personal data is processed
- **Data processor** — the entity that processes data on behalf of the controller

## 💻 Computer-Related Privacy Problems

Computers have fundamentally changed privacy by enabling:

### 1. Massive Data Collection
- Digital transactions leave trails — every click, purchase, search, and location is logged
- **Data aggregation** — combining data from multiple sources to create comprehensive profiles
- **Data persistence** — digital data can exist forever (unlike paper records that degrade)
- The **cost of storage** has dropped to near zero, so organizations keep everything "just in case"

### 2. Ease of Data Sharing and Merging
- Databases can be **linked** across organizations (e.g., credit bureaus, marketing firms, government agencies)
- **Data brokers** collect and sell personal information from public and commercial sources
- Once data is shared, it's nearly impossible to **recall or control** its further distribution

### 3. Unauthorized Access and Data Breaches
- Centralized databases create high-value targets for hackers
- **Insider threats** — employees with access can abuse it
- **Data breaches** have become endemic — millions of records exposed annually
- Stolen data can lead to **identity theft**, financial fraud, and personal harm

### 4. Surveillance Capabilities
- **Location tracking** — GPS, cell tower triangulation, Wi-Fi positioning
- **Communication monitoring** — email, phone, messaging interception capabilities
- **Video surveillance** — CCTV networks with facial recognition
- **Online behavioral tracking** — cookies, browser fingerprinting, analytics tools
- **IoT devices** — smart speakers, fitness trackers, connected home devices constantly generate data

### 5. Profiling and Automated Decision-Making
- Algorithms make decisions that affect people's lives (credit scores, hiring, insurance, policing)
- **Bias in algorithms** — training data may reflect historical discrimination
- Individuals often have **no knowledge** of these automated decisions and **no way to challenge** them
- **Filter bubbles** — personalization algorithms limit exposure to diverse viewpoints

### 6. Secondary Use of Data
- Data collected for one purpose is used for another without consent
- Example: health data collected for treatment used for insurance pricing
- Example: social media data used for political profiling
- This violates the principle of **purpose limitation**

### 7. Difficulty of True Anonymization
- **Re-identification attacks** — combining "anonymous" datasets with external data to identify individuals
- **Quasi-identifiers** — combinations of seemingly innocuous data (ZIP code + birthdate + gender) can uniquely identify most people
- Simply removing names is **not sufficient** for true anonymization

---

## 💡 Key Takeaways

> [!tip] Remember
> - Privacy is **multidimensional** — it's about control, anonymity, freedom from surveillance, and more
> - Computers create privacy problems through **massive collection, easy sharing, persistence, and aggregation**
> - **PII** is any data that can identify a person; it needs protection
> - **Secondary use** — using data beyond its original purpose — is a major privacy violation
> - True **anonymization** is extremely difficult due to re-identification risks
> - Privacy must be **balanced** against security, public interest, and commercial needs
