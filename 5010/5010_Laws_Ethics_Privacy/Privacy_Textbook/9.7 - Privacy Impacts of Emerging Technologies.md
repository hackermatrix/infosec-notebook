# 9.7 — Privacy Impacts of Emerging Technologies

> **Core Idea:** Emerging technologies continually create new privacy challenges. RFID, electronic voting, VoIP, and cloud computing each introduce unique threats to personal privacy that existing laws and frameworks struggle to address.

---

## 📡 Radio Frequency Identification (RFID)

### What Is RFID?
RFID uses electromagnetic fields to **automatically identify and track tags** attached to objects, animals, or people. Tags contain electronically stored information that can be read wirelessly by RFID readers.

### Types of RFID Tags

| Type | Power Source | Range | Cost | Example Use |
|------|-------------|-------|------|-------------|
| **Passive** | Powered by the reader's signal | Up to ~10 meters | Very low (cents) | Retail inventory, access badges, passports |
| **Active** | Has its own battery | Up to 100+ meters | Higher ($5-$100+) | Vehicle tracking, asset management |
| **Semi-passive** | Battery for chip; reader's signal for communication | Moderate | Moderate | Temperature monitoring in supply chain |

### Privacy Concerns with RFID

| Concern | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Covert tracking** | RFID tags can be read without the person's knowledge — no line-of-sight needed |
| **Inventorying possessions** | Someone with a reader could scan what items you carry (medications, books, products) |
| **Location tracking** | Fixed readers in stores, buildings, or public spaces could track your movements |
| **Data linkage** | Tag IDs can be linked to purchase records, tying products to individuals |
| **Passport and ID scanning** | E-passports contain RFID chips; can be skimmed from a distance |
| **Lack of consumer awareness** | Many people don't know products contain RFID tags |
| **Post-purchase tracking** | Tags remain active after purchase unless deliberately destroyed |

### RFID Privacy Protections
- **Kill commands** — permanently disable tags at point of sale
- **Faraday cages/sleeves** — block RFID signals (RFID-blocking wallets for passports and cards)
- **Blocker tags** — emit interfering signals to prevent scanning
- **Encryption** — encrypt data on tags so unauthorized readers can't interpret it
- **Consumer notification** — label products that contain RFID tags
- **Authentication protocols** — require authorized readers to identify themselves before tags respond

## 🗳️ Electronic Voting

### Types of Electronic Voting

| Type | Description |
|------|-------------|
| **DRE (Direct Recording Electronic)** | Touchscreen voting machines that record votes electronically |
| **Optical scan** | Paper ballots scanned and counted by machine |
| **Internet voting** | Voting via the internet from any location |

### Privacy Requirements for Voting
- **Ballot secrecy** — no one should be able to determine how a specific person voted
- **Coercion resistance** — the system should prevent vote-buying or forced voting
- **Verifiability** — voters should be able to verify their vote was counted correctly
- **Anonymity** — the link between voter identity and vote choice must be severed

### Privacy and Security Concerns

| Concern | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **No paper trail** | Some DRE machines have no paper backup — impossible to audit or recount |
| **Vote linkage risk** | Electronic systems may inadvertently link voter identity to vote choice through timestamps, sequence numbers, or logs |
| **Tampering** | Malware on voting machines could alter votes undetectably |
| **Internet voting risks** | Authentication is difficult; vulnerable to malware on voter's device, man-in-the-middle attacks, server attacks |
| **Vendor trust** | Proprietary, closed-source voting software can't be publicly audited |
| **Coercion** | Internet and remote voting makes coercion easier (someone watching you vote) |

### Protections
- **Voter-Verified Paper Audit Trail (VVPAT)** — paper receipt that voter can check before casting
- **Risk-limiting audits** — statistically rigorous post-election audits
- **Open-source software** — allows public inspection and trust
- **End-to-end verifiable systems** — cryptographic techniques that allow verification without compromising secrecy

## 📞 VoIP and Skype

### What Is VoIP?
**Voice over Internet Protocol (VoIP)** transmits voice communication over the internet rather than traditional phone lines. Examples include Skype, Zoom, WhatsApp calls, Teams, and Google Voice.

### Privacy Advantages of VoIP
- **Encryption** — many VoIP services encrypt calls (Skype, WhatsApp use end-to-end encryption for calls)
- **Harder to wiretap** — traditional phone taps don't work on VoIP; requires different techniques
- **International calls** bypass country-specific wiretapping infrastructure

### Privacy Concerns with VoIP

| Concern | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Provider access** | VoIP providers may have access to call content or metadata |
| **CALEA compliance** | U.S. law requires telecom providers (including some VoIP) to build in wiretapping capability for law enforcement |
| **Metadata collection** | Who called whom, when, duration — all logged |
| **Encryption variability** | Not all VoIP services encrypt calls; some encrypt only in transit, not end-to-end |
| **Quality of encryption** | Encryption strength and implementation varies by provider |
| **Malware on endpoints** | If your device is compromised, encryption doesn't matter — audio is captured before/after encryption |
| **Location data** | VoIP apps on phones can expose location information |

## ☁️ Privacy in the Cloud

### What Is Cloud Computing?
Cloud computing delivers computing resources (storage, processing, applications) over the internet, managed by third-party providers.

### Cloud Service Models

| Model | Description | Privacy Implication |
|-------|-------------|-------------------|
| **SaaS** (Software as a Service) | Applications hosted by provider (Gmail, Salesforce, Office 365) | Provider can access application data |
| **PaaS** (Platform as a Service) | Development platforms hosted by provider (Google App Engine, Heroku) | Provider can access platform data |
| **IaaS** (Infrastructure as a Service) | Virtual machines and storage (AWS, Azure, Google Cloud) | Customer has more control; provider has physical access |

### Privacy Concerns with Cloud Computing

| Concern | Description |
|---------|-------------|
| **Data location** | Your data may be stored in any country — subject to that country's laws |
| **Provider access** | Cloud providers have technical ability to access your data |
| **Government requests** | Providers may be compelled to hand over data by government orders (even foreign governments) |
| **Data commingling** | Your data is stored alongside other customers' data — isolation failures could expose data |
| **Lock-in and portability** | Difficulty extracting your data if you want to leave a provider |
| **Breach liability** | Who is responsible when a breach occurs — you or the provider? |
| **Subcontractors** | Providers may use subcontractors who also access your data |
| **Data persistence** | Deleted data may persist in backups, snapshots, and replicas |
| **Jurisdictional conflicts** | EU data stored on U.S. servers may be subject to conflicting privacy requirements |

### Key Legal Issue: Cross-Border Data Transfers
- **GDPR** restricts transfer of EU personal data outside the EU unless adequate protections exist
- **Schrems II decision** (2020) invalidated the EU-U.S. Privacy Shield framework
- **EU-U.S. Data Privacy Framework** (2023) — new framework to facilitate transatlantic data transfers
- Organizations must implement **Standard Contractual Clauses (SCCs)** or other approved mechanisms

### Cloud Privacy Best Practices
- **Encrypt data before uploading** — client-side encryption keeps provider out
- **Understand the provider's privacy policy** and data handling practices
- **Know where your data is stored** — geographic location matters for jurisdiction
- **Use strong authentication** — MFA for cloud accounts
- **Review access logs** — monitor who accesses your cloud data
- **Understand the shared responsibility model** — know what the provider secures vs. what you must secure
- **Have a data exit strategy** — ensure you can extract your data if needed

## 📝 Conclusions on Emerging Technologies

| Technology | Primary Privacy Threat | Key Protection |
|-----------|----------------------|----------------|
| **RFID** | Covert tracking and inventorying | Kill commands, Faraday sleeves, encryption |
| **E-Voting** | Vote secrecy compromise, tampering | Paper audit trails, open-source, end-to-end verification |
| **VoIP** | Metadata collection, variable encryption | End-to-end encrypted services, endpoint security |
| **Cloud** | Data jurisdiction, provider access, government requests | Client-side encryption, know your provider, understand data location |

**Common thread:** Emerging technologies are deployed faster than privacy protections and laws can adapt. **Privacy by design** — building privacy into systems from the start — is essential.

---

## 💡 Key Takeaways

> [!tip] Remember
> - **RFID** can track people covertly; **Faraday sleeves** and **kill commands** are key defenses
> - E-voting must balance **ballot secrecy, verifiability, and coercion resistance** — paper audit trails are essential
> - **VoIP** is harder to wiretap than traditional phones but still has privacy risks (metadata, provider access, CALEA)
> - **Cloud privacy** depends on **data location, provider policies, and encryption** — client-side encryption is the strongest protection
> - **Privacy by design** must be applied to new technologies before deployment, not after problems emerge
