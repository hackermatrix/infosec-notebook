# 11.3 — Rights of Employees and Employers

> **Core Idea:** When employees create software, inventions, or other intellectual property during their employment, questions of ownership and rights arise. Employment contracts, work-for-hire doctrine, and non-compete agreements define the boundaries.

---

## 🏢 Ownership of Products

### The Central Question
**Who owns the intellectual property created by an employee?**

### Work-for-Hire Doctrine

Under U.S. copyright law, when an employee creates a work **within the scope of their employment**, the employer is considered the author and owns the copyright. This is called **"work made for hire."**

#### Factors Determining Scope of Employment
- Was the work created during working hours?
- Was it created using the employer's resources (equipment, facilities)?
- Was it created as part of the employee's job duties?
- Was the employee hired to create this type of work?

#### Work-for-Hire Rules

| Situation | Who Owns It? |
|-----------|-------------|
| Employee creates software as part of their job duties | **Employer** — work for hire |
| Employee creates software at home, on their own time, unrelated to job | **Employee** — unless contract says otherwise |
| Employee creates software at home but related to employer's business | **Disputed** — depends on employment contract and circumstances |
| Independent contractor creates software | **Contractor** — unless there's a written work-for-hire agreement OR assignment of rights |
| Employee uses employer's resources for personal project | **Disputed** — employer may claim ownership |

### Inventions and Patents
- Many employers require employees to sign **invention assignment agreements**
- These typically assign rights to **any invention related to the employer's business** made during employment
- Some states (California, Delaware, Washington, and others) limit what employers can claim — generally excluding inventions made entirely on the employee's own time with their own resources and unrelated to the employer's business
- **Prior inventions** — employees should document pre-existing inventions to avoid disputes

## 📄 Employment Contracts

### Types of Employment Agreements Affecting IP and Security

| Agreement | Purpose | Typical Terms |
|-----------|---------|---------------|
| **Non-Disclosure Agreement (NDA)** | Prevents employees from sharing confidential information | Defines what's confidential; specifies duration; outlines consequences of breach |
| **Non-Compete Agreement** | Restricts employee from working for competitors after leaving | Specifies geographic area, duration, and industry scope |
| **Invention Assignment Agreement** | Assigns IP rights created during employment to the employer | Covers inventions, software, designs related to employer's business |
| **Acceptable Use Policy (AUP)** | Defines how employees may use company systems and data | Email monitoring, internet use, personal device policies |
| **Separation / Exit Agreement** | Defines obligations upon leaving (return of materials, ongoing confidentiality) | Return of equipment, data destruction, non-solicitation |

### Non-Compete Agreements — Key Issues

| Issue | Detail |
|-------|--------|
| **Enforceability** | Varies widely by jurisdiction — some states (California) refuse to enforce them; others require reasonableness |
| **Reasonableness** | Must be reasonable in scope (geographic area), duration (typically 1-2 years), and activity restricted |
| **Consideration** | Must have something in exchange — employment itself may suffice for new hires; existing employees may need additional compensation |
| **Overly broad** | Courts may strike down or narrow agreements that are too restrictive |
| **FTC rule (2024)** | FTC issued a rule banning most non-competes (enforcement is subject to legal challenges) |

### Employee Monitoring
- Employers generally have the **right to monitor** company-owned systems (email, internet, phone)
- **Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA)** — provides some protections but has broad employer exceptions
- Best practice: **inform employees** that monitoring occurs (through policy and acknowledgment)
- Some jurisdictions require **explicit notice** of monitoring

### Employee Departure — Security Concerns
- **Immediately revoke all access** upon departure (network, email, physical access)
- Ensure return of all **company property** (laptops, badges, documents, storage media)
- Remind departing employees of **ongoing NDA and confidentiality obligations**
- Conduct an **exit interview** addressing security obligations
- Monitor for **data exfiltration** before and after departure
- Some organizations use **garden leave** — paying employees to stay home during their notice period to prevent them from accessing sensitive information

---

## 💡 Key Takeaways

> [!tip] Remember
> - **Work-for-hire** — employer owns IP created within the scope of employment
> - **Independent contractors** own their work unless there's a written agreement assigning rights
> - **NDAs** protect confidential information; **non-competes** restrict future employment (enforceability varies)
> - **Invention assignment agreements** are standard in tech — assign job-related inventions to the employer
> - **Employee monitoring** is generally legal on company systems with proper notice
> - **Immediate access revocation** upon employee departure is critical security practice
