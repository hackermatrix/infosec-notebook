# 11.7 — Incident Analysis with Ethics

> **Core Idea:** This section applies ethical reasoning to concrete scenarios, demonstrating how the frameworks from [[11.6 - Ethical Issues in Computer Security]] work in practice. Each situation involves competing interests, ambiguous rules, and no single "right" answer — exactly the kind of problems real security professionals face.

---

## 🔍 Situation I: Use of Computer Services

### Scenario
A student uses their university's computer to run a personal side business. The university provides computing resources for educational purposes.

### Ethical Analysis
| Framework | Perspective |
|-----------|------------|
| **Deontology** | Using resources for unauthorized purposes violates the terms of access — it's wrong regardless of whether it causes measurable harm |
| **Consequentialism** | If the usage is minimal and doesn't impact other students, the harm is small — but it still diverts shared resources |
| **Rights** | Other students have a right to the resources they're paying for through tuition |
| **Virtue** | An honest person would ask permission rather than assume it's acceptable |

**Key Issues:** Acceptable use policies exist for a reason. Even minor unauthorized use establishes a precedent and erodes trust. The scale of use matters ethically — browsing a personal email is different from running a commercial server.

---

## 🔍 Situation II: Privacy Rights

### Scenario
A system administrator discovers evidence of employee misconduct while performing routine maintenance — not through targeted surveillance.

### Ethical Analysis
| Framework | Perspective |
|-----------|------------|
| **Rights** | Employees have some expectation of privacy; accidental discovery is different from targeted surveillance |
| **Consequentialism** | Reporting could prevent harm to the organization; ignoring it could enable continued misconduct |
| **Deontology** | Admin has a duty to report policy violations, but also a duty to respect privacy |
| **Professional** | Follow organizational policy on reporting; avoid overstepping authority |

**Key Issues:** What is the admin's obligation? They weren't looking for misconduct. How they handle this affects trust in the entire IT department. The severity of the misconduct matters — criminal activity vs. minor policy violation.

---

## 🔍 Situation III: Denial of Service

### Scenario
A network administrator discovers they could shut down a competing organization's service by exploiting a known vulnerability.

### Ethical Analysis
- **Clearly unethical and illegal** under virtually every framework
- Violates CFAA (unauthorized access/damage), professional codes of ethics, and basic moral principles
- Knowledge of a vulnerability creates a **responsibility to use it ethically** (report it), not exploit it
- The fact that you *can* do something doesn't mean you *should*

---

## 🔍 Situation IV: Ownership of Programs

### Scenario
A developer writes a utility program at work using company resources. The program is useful but not related to their job duties. Who owns it?

### Ethical Analysis
| Framework | Perspective |
|-----------|------------|
| **Legal** | Depends on the employment contract — work-for-hire doctrine may give the employer ownership |
| **Fairness** | The employee created it with their own creativity; the employer provided the resources |
| **Consequentialism** | Clear policies prevent disputes; ambiguity harms both parties |
| **Social contract** | Employee accepted terms of employment that likely addressed IP ownership |

**Key Issues:** This is why clear **invention assignment agreements** matter. Ethically, the developer should be transparent with their employer rather than secretly claiming ownership.

---

## 🔍 Situation V: Proprietary Resources

### Scenario
An employee leaves a company and uses knowledge gained there to develop a competing product — without taking any physical materials or copying code.

### Ethical Analysis
| Framework | Perspective |
|-----------|------------|
| **Legal** | General skills and knowledge are the employee's; trade secrets and confidential information are not |
| **Rights** | Employee has the right to earn a living; employer has the right to protect secrets |
| **Deontology** | Honoring NDAs and confidentiality obligations is a duty |
| **Consequentialism** | Innovation benefits from mobility of talent; but theft of secrets harms companies and discourages R&D investment |

**Key Issues:** The line between **general knowledge/skills** (which the employee owns) and **trade secrets** (which belong to the employer) is often blurry and contentious.

---

## 🔍 Situation VI: Fraud

### Scenario
A programmer discovers they could manipulate financial calculations in software to divert small amounts (fractions of cents) to their own account — the classic "salami slicing" attack.

### Ethical Analysis
- **Clearly unethical and illegal** — theft, fraud, unauthorized modification
- The small amounts don't make it less wrong — **scale doesn't change the moral character**
- Violates every ethical framework: duties (deontology), outcomes (total harm), rights (property rights), character (dishonesty)
- Classic example of **rationalizing** unethical behavior ("it's so small no one will notice")

---

## 🔍 Situation VII: Accuracy of Information

### Scenario
A developer discovers that a system is producing slightly inaccurate results but the errors are small and fixing them would be expensive and delay an important release.

### Ethical Analysis
| Framework | Perspective |
|-----------|------------|
| **Consequentialism** | Depends on context — small errors in a game vs. small errors in medical dosage calculations are very different |
| **Deontology** | Users deserve accurate systems; there's a duty of honesty |
| **Professional** | Professional codes require competent, diligent service |
| **Rights** | Users have a right to know about limitations and inaccuracies |

**Key Issues:** The **context and potential consequences** of the inaccuracy determine the ethical obligation. Transparency is key — at minimum, users should be informed of known limitations.

---

## 🔍 Situation VIII: Ethics of Hacking or Cracking

### Scenario
A security researcher breaks into a system without authorization to test its security, intending to report vulnerabilities to the owner.

### Ethical Analysis
| Framework | Perspective |
|-----------|------------|
| **Consequentialism** | The outcome (improved security) may be positive, but the means (unauthorized access) creates risks |
| **Deontology** | Unauthorized access violates rules regardless of intent — the end doesn't justify the means |
| **Legal** | Illegal under CFAA regardless of motive |
| **Professional** | Penetration testing requires **explicit authorization** — ethical hackers get permission first |

**Key Issues:** "White hat" hacking without permission is still **unauthorized access**. The ethical approach is to seek permission or use responsible disclosure. Good intentions don't create authorization.

---

## 🔍 Situation IX: True Representation

### Scenario
A security vendor exaggerates the capabilities of their product or downplays known limitations in marketing materials.

### Ethical Analysis
- Violates **honesty** — a core virtue and duty
- **Consequentialism** — customers may make security decisions based on false information, leaving them vulnerable
- **Professional ethics** — misleading claims undermine trust in the entire industry
- May constitute **fraud or misrepresentation** legally
- Particularly harmful because customers **rely on security products** to protect them

---

## 📝 Conclusion of Computer Ethics

### Recurring Themes Across All Scenarios

1. **Intent matters, but doesn't excuse everything** — good intentions don't make unauthorized access legal or ethical
2. **Transparency and honesty** are almost always the ethical path
3. **Context determines severity** — the same action can be minor or catastrophic depending on circumstances
4. **Multiple frameworks often agree** — if an action fails multiple ethical tests, it's almost certainly wrong
5. **The law is the floor, not the ceiling** — ethical behavior exceeds legal requirements
6. **Power creates responsibility** — security professionals have access that creates ethical obligations
7. **When in doubt, ask** — seek permission, consult policies, get advice

### Practical Ethical Guidelines for Security Professionals
- Always get **explicit authorization** before testing systems
- **Report vulnerabilities** through proper channels
- **Respect privacy** — access only what you need to do your job
- Be **honest** about capabilities and limitations
- **Follow professional codes** of ethics (ACM, (ISC)², etc.)
- Consider the **impact on all stakeholders**, not just the immediate parties
- **Document** decisions and reasoning for accountability
- When legal and ethical obligations conflict, seek counsel

## 🎯 Key Exam Concepts

| Concept | Key Detail |
|---------|------------|
| **Copyright** | Protects expression (code), not ideas; automatic; life + 70 years |
| **Patent** | Protects inventions/algorithms; 20 years; requires registration and disclosure |
| **Trade secret** | Protects confidential info; lasts forever if secret maintained; no registration |
| **CFAA** | Primary U.S. computer crime law; "protected computer" = any internet-connected computer |
| **Budapest Convention** | First international cybercrime treaty |
| **Work-for-hire** | Employer owns IP created within scope of employment |
| **Fair use** | Four factors: purpose, nature, amount, market effect |
| **DMCA** | Criminalizes circumventing DRM/copy protection |
| **Responsible disclosure** | Notify vendor → allow time to fix → then disclose publicly |
| **Legal ≠ Ethical** | Law is the minimum; ethics aims higher |
| **Consequentialism** | Greatest good for the greatest number |
| **Deontology** | Rules and duties, regardless of outcomes |
| **Virtue ethics** | What would a person of good character do? |
| **ACM Code** | Contribute to society, avoid harm, be honest, respect privacy |
| **(ISC)² Code** | Protect society, act honorably, provide competent service, advance profession |
| **Negligence** | Failure to exercise reasonable care |
| **Strict liability** | Rarely applied to software |
| **EULA** | Typically disclaims warranties and limits liability ("AS IS") |
