# 11.5 — Computer Crime

> **Core Idea:** Computer crime is a unique and challenging area of law. Traditional criminal statutes were not designed for digital offenses, making separate legislation necessary. Defining, prosecuting, and catching computer criminals is difficult due to the nature of technology, jurisdictional issues, and the speed at which the landscape evolves.

---

## ❓ Why a Separate Category for Computer Crime Is Needed

Traditional criminal laws were written for **physical acts** — theft of physical objects, trespassing on physical property, forgery of physical documents. Computer crimes don't fit neatly:

| Traditional Crime | Computer Equivalent | Why Traditional Law Falls Short |
|------------------|--------------------|---------------------------------|
| Theft | Copying proprietary data | Original owner still has the data — is it "theft"? |
| Trespassing | Unauthorized system access | No physical entry — where's the "property line"? |
| Vandalism | Deleting files, defacing websites | Damage is to intangible data, not physical property |
| Wiretapping | Network sniffing, packet capture | Different technology requires different legal frameworks |
| Forgery | Email spoofing, digital fraud | No physical document — traditional forgery laws may not apply |
| Breaking and entering | Bypassing authentication | No physical lock was broken |

**Separate computer crime laws** are needed because:
- Traditional statutes don't clearly cover **intangible harm**
- **Damage** can be massive but purely digital
- **Perpetrators can act remotely** across jurisdictions
- The **speed and scale** of computer crime is unlike traditional crime
- Courts and juries may not understand the technical aspects without specific legal framework

---

## 🔍 Why Computer Crime Is Hard to Define

### The Definition Challenge
- **What constitutes "unauthorized access"?** — If a website has no login page, is visiting it unauthorized?
- **What's a "computer"?** — Smartphones, IoT devices, embedded systems, cars?
- **When does exploring become crime?** — Finding a vulnerability vs. exploiting it
- **Mens rea (criminal intent)** — Was the action intentional, reckless, or accidental?
- **Proportionality** — accessing a public website vs. hacking a classified system are vastly different, but the same statute may cover both

### The Breadth Problem
The **CFAA**, for example, has been criticized as **overly broad** — potentially criminalizing:
- Violating a website's Terms of Service
- Using a work computer for personal tasks
- Sharing passwords
- Aaron Swartz case — aggressive prosecution for downloading academic articles

---

## 🏛️ Why Computer Crime Is Hard to Prosecute

| Challenge | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Attribution** | Identifying who committed the crime — attackers use proxies, VPNs, Tor, compromised machines |
| **Evidence volatility** | Digital evidence can be easily altered or destroyed; requires proper forensic procedures |
| **Jurisdiction** | Attacker in one country, victim in another, servers in a third — whose laws apply? |
| **Technical complexity** | Prosecutors, judges, and juries may lack technical understanding |
| **Victim reluctance** | Organizations may not report crimes to avoid reputational damage |
| **Resource disparity** | Law enforcement may lack the technical resources to investigate sophisticated attacks |
| **Statute of limitations** | Crimes may not be discovered until well after the fact |
| **Evidence admissibility** | Digital evidence must meet strict chain-of-custody requirements |
| **Encryption** | Encrypted data and communications can block investigation |
| **International cooperation** | Requires treaties and cooperation between countries (often slow or unavailable) |

## 📜 Examples of Statutes

### U.S. Federal Computer Crime Laws

| Statute                                                    | What It Covers                                                                                                                                                                                                               |
| ---------------------------------------------------------- | ---------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------------- |
| **Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA) — 18 U.S.C. § 1030** | Primary federal computer crime law; prohibits unauthorized access to computers, exceeding authorized access, trafficking in passwords, causing damage, fraud via computer; covers both federal and certain private computers |
| **Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA)**           | Prohibits unauthorized interception of electronic communications; covers wiretapping, stored communications, pen registers                                                                                                   |
| **Identity Theft and Assumption Deterrence Act**           | Makes identity theft a federal crime                                                                                                                                                                                         |
| **CAN-SPAM Act**                                           | Regulates commercial email; penalties for fraudulent headers, deceptive subjects                                                                                                                                             |
| **Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA)**                | Criminalizes circumventing copyright protection technology                                                                                                                                                                   |
| **Economic Espionage Act**                                 | Criminalizes theft of trade secrets for foreign governments or commercial advantage                                                                                                                                          |
| **Wire Fraud (18 U.S.C. § 1343)**                          | Fraud conducted via electronic communications — broadly applicable to many cyber schemes                                                                                                                                     |
| **Stored Communications Act (part of ECPA)**               | Governs access to stored electronic communications                                                                                                                                                                           |

### CFAA in Detail
The CFAA is the **most important U.S. computer crime statute**. It prohibits:
1. Unauthorized access to **government computers** to obtain classified information
2. Unauthorized access to obtain **financial records** or credit information
3. Unauthorized access to **federal government computers**
4. **Fraud** via unauthorized computer access
5. Knowingly causing **damage** to a protected computer
6. **Trafficking in passwords** or similar access credentials
7. **Extortion** involving threats to a computer

**"Protected computer"** = essentially any computer connected to the internet (very broad).

## 🌍 International Dimensions

### Cross-Border Challenges
- Cyberattacks routinely **cross national boundaries**
- No single country has jurisdiction over the entire internet
- **Extradition** requires treaties and cooperation
- Some countries are **safe havens** for cybercriminals (lack of laws or enforcement)
- Evidence may be stored in a **different country** than the attacker or victim

### International Frameworks
| Framework | Description |
|-----------|-------------|
| **Budapest Convention on Cybercrime (2001)** | First international treaty on cybercrime; harmonizes definitions and procedures; requires cooperation in investigations; signed by 60+ countries |
| **Mutual Legal Assistance Treaties (MLATs)** | Bilateral agreements for cross-border evidence sharing — often slow (months to years) |
| **CLOUD Act (U.S., 2018)** | Allows U.S. law enforcement to compel U.S. tech companies to provide data stored abroad; allows bilateral agreements for faster data access |
| **Europol / EC3** | EU's cybercrime center — coordinates cross-border investigations within the EU |
| **Interpol** | Facilitates international police cooperation on cybercrime |

## 🕵️ Why Computer Criminals Are Hard to Catch

| Factor | Description |
|--------|-------------|
| **Anonymity tools** | VPNs, Tor, proxy chains, compromised machines as stepping stones |
| **Spoofed identities** | Fake email addresses, stolen credentials, synthetic identities |
| **Global reach** | Attacker in one country, victim in another, infrastructure in a third |
| **Speed** | Attacks happen in milliseconds; evidence can disappear instantly |
| **Encrypted communications** | Makes surveillance and evidence gathering difficult |
| **Cryptocurrency** | Anonymous or pseudonymous payments for services and ransoms |
| **Sophistication** | Advanced persistent threats (APTs) use custom tools and advanced tradecraft |
| **Volume** | Millions of attacks daily — impossible to investigate them all |
| **Insider access** | Insiders may leave fewer traces than external attackers |
| **Dark web** | Marketplaces for tools, stolen data, and criminal services are hidden |

## ⚠️ What Computer Crime Does Not Address

Computer crime law has **significant gaps**:
- **Ethics** — law defines minimum acceptable behavior; ethics goes further (see [[11.6 - Ethical Issues in Computer Security]])
- **Negligent security** — failing to protect systems is often not criminal (but may be civilly liable)
- **State-sponsored attacks** — nation-state cyber operations exist in a legal gray zone
- **Emerging technologies** — laws lag behind AI, IoT, quantum computing threats
- **Proportionality** — minor violations (ToS breaches) may face same statutes as serious attacks
- **Victim compensation** — criminal prosecution doesn't automatically compensate victims
- **Prevention** — criminal law is reactive (punishes after the fact), not proactive

## 📊 Summary of Legal Issues in Computer Security

| Area | Key Statute/Concept | Primary Issue |
|------|---------------------|---------------|
| Unauthorized access | CFAA | Defining "authorization" |
| Interception | ECPA/Wiretap Act | Balancing surveillance and privacy |
| IP theft | DMCA, Economic Espionage Act | Protecting digital intellectual property |
| Identity theft | Identity Theft Act | Protecting personal information |
| Fraud | Wire Fraud, CFAA | Applying fraud concepts to digital context |
| International crime | Budapest Convention, CLOUD Act | Cross-border jurisdiction and cooperation |
| Evidence | Federal Rules of Evidence | Digital evidence handling and admissibility |

---

## 💡 Key Takeaways

> [!tip] Remember
> - Separate computer crime laws are needed because **traditional statutes don't cover intangible digital harm**
> - **CFAA** is the primary U.S. computer crime law — prohibits unauthorized access and damage to "protected computers"
> - Computer crime is hard to **define** (what's "unauthorized"?), **prosecute** (jurisdiction, evidence), and **solve** (anonymity, speed)
> - **Budapest Convention** is the first international cybercrime treaty
> - **Attribution** is the biggest investigation challenge — anonymity tools make identification extremely difficult
> - Criminal law has **gaps** — it doesn't address ethics, prevention, or many emerging technology threats
