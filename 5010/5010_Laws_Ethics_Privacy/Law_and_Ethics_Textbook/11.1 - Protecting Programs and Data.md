# 11.1 — Protecting Programs and Data

> **Core Idea:** Intellectual property (IP) law provides the legal mechanisms to protect software, data, and digital creations. The three primary tools — copyrights, patents, and trade secrets — each offer different types of protection with different strengths, limitations, and durations.

---

## ©️ Copyrights

### What Copyright Protects
Copyright protects the **expression** of an idea — not the idea itself. For software, this means the **specific code** (source and object code), the structure, documentation, and user interface elements are protectable, but the **underlying algorithm or concept** is not.

### Key Characteristics

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **What's protected** | The specific expression — source code, object code, screen displays, documentation, user interface elements |
| **What's NOT protected** | Ideas, algorithms, concepts, methods, facts, procedures |
| **Duration** | Life of the author + 70 years (individual); 95 years from publication or 120 years from creation (works for hire) |
| **Registration required?** | No — copyright exists automatically upon creation in a fixed form; registration strengthens legal position |
| **Symbol** | © (not required but recommended) |
| **Scope** | Prevents unauthorized copying, distribution, display, and creation of derivative works |

### The Idea vs. Expression Dichotomy
This is a **critical concept**: copyright protects HOW something is expressed, not WHAT is expressed.

- ✅ Protected: The specific Java code implementing a sorting algorithm
- ❌ Not protected: The sorting algorithm itself (others can implement the same algorithm in different code)
- ✅ Protected: The specific wording and layout of a user manual
- ❌ Not protected: The factual information in the manual

### Fair Use Doctrine
**Fair use** allows limited use of copyrighted material without permission for:
- **Criticism and commentary**
- **News reporting**
- **Teaching and scholarship**
- **Research**
- **Parody**

Four factors courts consider:
1. **Purpose and character** of the use (commercial vs. educational; transformative vs. copying)
2. **Nature** of the copyrighted work (factual vs. creative)
3. **Amount and substantiality** of the portion used
4. **Effect on the market** for the original work

### Software-Specific Copyright Issues
- **Reverse engineering** — courts have sometimes allowed decompilation for interoperability
- **Look and feel** — court cases have debated whether UI design is copyrightable (Oracle v. Google addressed APIs)
- **Open source licenses** — GPL, MIT, Apache — use copyright to grant (not restrict) usage rights
- **Digital Millennium Copyright Act (DMCA)** — criminalizes circumventing technical copyright protection measures (DRM)

## 📜 Patents

### What a Patent Protects
A patent protects an **invention** — a novel, non-obvious, useful process, machine, manufacture, or composition of matter. For software, patents can protect algorithms, methods, and processes (unlike copyright).

### Key Characteristics

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **What's protected** | Inventions: novel processes, methods, algorithms, business methods, technical innovations |
| **Duration** | 20 years from filing date (utility patents) |
| **Registration required?** | Yes — must file with a patent office; examined and granted |
| **Requirements** | Must be **novel**, **non-obvious**, and **useful** |
| **Scope** | Prevents anyone from making, using, selling, or importing the invention without permission |
| **Cost** | Expensive — application, examination, attorney fees, maintenance fees |

### Patents vs. Copyright for Software

| Aspect | Copyright | Patent |
|--------|-----------|--------|
| **Protects** | Specific code (expression) | Algorithm/method (invention) |
| **Independent creation defense** | Yes — if you write the same code independently, no infringement | No — even independent invention infringes |
| **Duration** | Very long (life + 70 years) | Shorter (20 years) |
| **Cost** | Free (automatic) | Expensive |
| **Registration** | Optional | Required |
| **Disclosure** | Not required | Must publicly disclose the invention |
| **Strength against copying** | Prevents copying the specific code | Prevents anyone from using the method, even in different code |

### Controversial Aspects of Software Patents
- **Patent trolls** — entities that acquire patents solely to sue others for infringement
- **Broad/vague patents** — overly broad software patents can stifle innovation
- **Prior art** — patents sometimes granted for things that already existed
- **International inconsistency** — software patentability varies by country (EU is more restrictive than U.S.)

## 🤫 Trade Secrets

### What a Trade Secret Protects
A trade secret protects **confidential business information** that derives value from being secret.

### Key Characteristics

| Aspect | Detail |
|--------|--------|
| **What's protected** | Any information that has commercial value because it is secret — formulas, algorithms, customer lists, processes, source code |
| **Duration** | Potentially forever — as long as the secret is maintained |
| **Registration required?** | No — protection exists as long as reasonable measures are taken to maintain secrecy |
| **Requirements** | Must be (1) secret, (2) have commercial value because it's secret, (3) subject to reasonable efforts to maintain secrecy |
| **Loss of protection** | If the secret becomes public (through disclosure, reverse engineering, or independent discovery), protection is lost |

### Maintaining Trade Secret Protection
- **Non-Disclosure Agreements (NDAs)** — contractual obligation to keep information confidential
- **Access controls** — limit who can access the information
- **Labeling** — mark documents as confidential/proprietary
- **Employee training** — educate employees about confidentiality obligations
- **Exit procedures** — remind departing employees of ongoing obligations
- **Physical and digital security** — locked facilities, encrypted storage

### Advantages and Disadvantages

| Advantage | Disadvantage |
|-----------|-------------|
| No registration or disclosure required | Lost forever if secret becomes public |
| Potentially infinite duration | No protection against independent discovery |
| Protects information that may not qualify for patent/copyright | No protection against reverse engineering (in most cases) |
| Immediate protection — no waiting period | Must prove reasonable secrecy measures were taken |
| No cost to obtain (unlike patents) | Difficult to license without risking disclosure |

## 🔍 Special Cases

### Software Licensing
- Software is typically **licensed, not sold** — the license agreement defines what users can do
- **End User License Agreements (EULAs)** — contracts between vendor and user
- **Shrink-wrap / Click-wrap / Browse-wrap** licenses — enforceability has been debated in courts
- **Open Source licenses** — use copyright to grant broad usage rights (GPL, MIT, BSD, Apache)

### Database Protection
- **Facts cannot be copyrighted** — databases of facts may not be protectable by copyright alone
- **Creative selection/arrangement** may qualify for copyright (Feist v. Rural Telephone)
- **EU Database Directive** — provides sui generis protection for database makers who invest substantially
- **Terms of service** — contractual protection for database access

### Reverse Engineering
- Generally **legal** for interoperability purposes (Sega v. Accolade, Sony v. Connectix)
- May be restricted by **contract** (EULAs, NDAs)
- **DMCA** may prohibit circumventing technical protection measures even for legitimate reverse engineering

### Digital Rights Management (DRM)
- Technical measures to control access to and use of copyrighted works
- DMCA makes it illegal to circumvent DRM, even for otherwise lawful purposes
- Creates tension with **fair use** rights
- Criticized for restricting legitimate uses and user rights

---

## 💡 Key Takeaways

> [!tip] Remember
> - **Copyright** protects expression (code); **Patent** protects invention (algorithm); **Trade secret** protects confidential information
> - Copyright is **automatic and free**; patents are **expensive and require registration**
> - Copyright has an **independent creation defense**; patents do **not**
> - Trade secrets last **forever** but are lost if the secret becomes public
> - The **idea vs. expression dichotomy** is fundamental — copyright doesn't protect ideas
> - **Fair use** allows limited copying for criticism, education, research, and parody
> - **DMCA** criminalizes circumventing copy-protection technology
