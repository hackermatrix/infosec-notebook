# 11.2 — Information and the Law

> **Core Idea:** Information doesn't fit neatly into traditional legal categories. It is intangible, easily copied, and hard to value — yet it's often the most valuable asset an organization has. The law has struggled to adapt traditional property and contract concepts to the unique nature of digital information.

---

## 📦 Information as an Object

### Why Information Is Legally Unique

Traditional property law deals with **tangible objects** — if I steal your car, you no longer have it. Information is fundamentally different:

| Property of Information | Legal Challenge |
|------------------------|-----------------|
| **Intangible** | Can't be "possessed" in the traditional sense |
| **Non-depleting** | Copying doesn't deprive the owner — they still have the original |
| **Easy to copy** | Perfect copies at near-zero cost; hard to prevent |
| **Hard to value** | What's the dollar value of a customer list? A trade secret? |
| **Hard to contain** | Once disclosed, impossible to "un-disclose" |
| **Context-dependent** | The same information may be public in one context, confidential in another |
| **Aggregation effect** | Individual data points may be harmless; combined, they're valuable or sensitive |

### Information and Property Law
- Traditional property concepts (**theft, trespass, conversion**) don't map cleanly to information
- Can you "steal" information if the owner still has their copy?
- Courts and legislatures have gradually extended legal protections to cover **unauthorized access to and copying of** digital information
- Information is increasingly treated as **a form of property**, but the framework remains imperfect

---

## ⚖️ Legal Issues Relating to Information

### Unauthorized Access
- Accessing a computer system without authorization is illegal under laws like the **Computer Fraud and Abuse Act (CFAA)**
- Even **exceeding authorized access** can be criminal
- Questions arise: What constitutes "authorization"? If a website is publicly accessible, is accessing it "unauthorized"?

### Unauthorized Modification
- Altering data without permission (defacing a website, modifying records, planting malware)
- Criminal under computer crime statutes
- Civil liability for damages caused by modification

### Unauthorized Disclosure
- Sharing confidential information with unauthorized parties
- Covered by trade secret law, NDAs, privacy laws, and employment agreements
- **Whistleblower protections** create exceptions for disclosing illegal activity

### Interception
- Intercepting electronic communications (email, phone, network traffic) is restricted by:
  - **Wiretap Act (Title III)**
  - **Electronic Communications Privacy Act (ECPA)**
  - **Foreign Intelligence Surveillance Act (FISA)**
- Exceptions exist for **law enforcement with warrants** and **service providers for network operations**

### Data Integrity
- Legal requirements to maintain accurate records (financial regulations, health records)
- Liability for **negligent data management** that leads to harm
- **Sarbanes-Oxley Act (SOX)** — requires companies to maintain accurate financial records and internal controls

## 🏛️ The Legal System

### Types of Law Relevant to Computer Security

| Type | Description | Example |
|------|-------------|---------|
| **Criminal law** | Government prosecutes violations; penalties include fines and imprisonment | CFAA violations, hacking, identity theft |
| **Civil law (Tort)** | Private parties sue for damages caused by wrongful acts | Suing a company for a data breach |
| **Contract law** | Enforces agreements between parties | EULAs, NDAs, service level agreements |
| **Administrative / Regulatory law** | Government agencies create and enforce rules | HIPAA regulations, FTC enforcement actions |
| **Constitutional law** | Fundamental rights and government powers | Fourth Amendment (search and seizure), First Amendment (free speech) |

### Key Legal Concepts

| Concept | Definition | Relevance |
|---------|-----------|-----------|
| **Jurisdiction** | Which court/authority has power to hear a case | Internet crimes may cross state/national boundaries |
| **Standing** | Right to bring a lawsuit (must show actual injury) | Data breach victims must prove harm |
| **Burden of proof** | Who must prove what — "beyond reasonable doubt" (criminal) vs. "preponderance of evidence" (civil) | Different standards for criminal vs. civil cases |
| **Due diligence** | Taking reasonable care to prevent harm | Organizations must show they took reasonable security measures |
| **Liability** | Legal responsibility for consequences | Who's liable when software fails or data is breached? |
| **Negligence** | Failure to exercise reasonable care | Not patching known vulnerabilities; not encrypting sensitive data |
| **Precedent** | Past court decisions that guide future cases | Computer law is evolving through case precedents |

## 📊 Summary of Protection for Computer Artifacts

| Artifact | Best Protection | Why |
|----------|----------------|-----|
| **Source code** | Copyright + Trade Secret | Copyright protects the expression; trade secret protects the code from being seen at all |
| **Object/compiled code** | Copyright | Automatically protected; reverse engineering may be limited by DMCA |
| **Algorithm** | Patent (if novel/non-obvious) or Trade Secret | Copyright doesn't protect algorithms; patent requires disclosure; trade secret avoids disclosure |
| **Data/Database** | Copyright (for creative arrangement) + Contract (Terms of Service) + Trade Secret | Facts themselves aren't copyrightable; creative arrangement may be |
| **User interface / Screen displays** | Copyright (limited) + Patent (for novel interaction methods) | "Look and feel" protection is limited after Lotus v. Borland |
| **Business method** | Patent | Software business methods may be patentable in the U.S. |
| **Documentation / Manuals** | Copyright | Standard literary work protection |
| **Brand / Product name** | Trademark | Protects names, logos, symbols that identify products |

---

## 💡 Key Takeaways

> [!tip] Remember
> - Information is **intangible, non-depleting, and easy to copy** — traditional property law doesn't fit perfectly
> - **Copying information doesn't deprive the owner** — this makes "theft" conceptually different
> - The legal system uses **criminal, civil, contract, and regulatory law** together to protect information
> - **Due diligence and negligence** are key concepts — organizations must take reasonable security precautions
> - Different computer artifacts need **different types of IP protection** — there's no one-size-fits-all
> - **Jurisdiction** is a major challenge for internet crimes that cross borders
