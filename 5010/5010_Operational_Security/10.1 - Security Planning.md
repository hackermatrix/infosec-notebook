# 10.1 : Security Planning

> **Core Idea:** A security plan is a formal document that defines an organization's security posture : what assets need protection, who is responsible, and what policies and controls are in place. Without a plan, security is ad hoc and inconsistent.

---

## 🏢 Organizations and Security Plans

### Why Organizations Need a Security Plan
- Security threats are **constant and evolving** : reactive approaches fail
- A plan provides a **shared framework** so all employees, managers, and IT staff understand their roles
- Regulatory and legal requirements (HIPAA, GDPR, SOX, PCI-DSS) often **mandate** documented security plans
- Insurance providers may require evidence of a formal plan
- A plan helps **prioritize** limited resources toward the most critical assets

### What a Security Plan Achieves
- **Defines scope** : what systems, data, and people are covered
- **Establishes accountability** : who owns which security responsibilities
- **Sets baseline controls** : minimum standards for passwords, access, encryption, etc.
- **Creates measurable goals** : metrics to evaluate effectiveness
- **Provides legal protection** : demonstrates due diligence

---

## 📄 Contents of a Security Plan

A comprehensive security plan typically includes:

### 1. Policy Statement
- High-level declaration of the organization's commitment to security
- Signed by senior leadership (CEO, CIO, Board)
- Sets the tone : "security is everyone's responsibility"

### 2. Scope and Objectives
- Which assets are covered (data, hardware, software, networks, people)
- Clear boundaries : what is **in scope** vs. out of scope
- Specific, measurable security objectives

### 3. Asset Inventory and Classification
- Catalog of all information assets
- Classification levels (e.g., Public, Internal, Confidential, Restricted)
- Data ownership assignments

### 4. Threat and Vulnerability Assessment
- Identified threats (internal and external)
- Known vulnerabilities in current systems
- Links to the organization's [[10.4 - Risk Analysis]]

### 5. Security Controls and Countermeasures
- **Administrative controls** : policies, training, background checks
- **Technical controls** : firewalls, IDS/IPS, encryption, access controls
- **Physical controls** : locks, badges, surveillance, environmental protections

### 6. Roles and Responsibilities
- Who does what (see Team Members below)
- Reporting structure for security issues
- Escalation procedures

### 7. Incident Response Procedures
- Cross-reference to [[10.3 - Handling Incidents]]
- How to report, classify, and respond to incidents

### 8. Training and Awareness Program
- Security awareness training schedule
- Role-specific training (admins, developers, end users)
- Phishing simulations and testing

### 9. Compliance Requirements
- Applicable laws, regulations, and standards
- Audit schedule and procedures

### 10. Review and Update Schedule
- How often the plan is reviewed (typically annually or after major incidents)
- Change management process for the plan itself

## 👥 Security Planning Team Members

Building a security plan is **not** a solo effort. It requires a cross-functional team:

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| **Executive Sponsor** (CIO/CISO) | Provides authority, budget, and organizational commitment |
| **Security Manager / CISO** | Leads the planning process; owns the plan |
| **IT Staff / System Admins** | Provide technical expertise on systems, networks, and vulnerabilities |
| **Legal Counsel** | Ensures compliance with laws, regulations, and contractual obligations |
| **HR Representative** | Addresses personnel policies, background checks, termination procedures |
| **Department Managers** | Represent business unit needs; identify critical assets within their domain |
| **End-User Representatives** | Provide perspective on usability and day-to-day security challenges |
| **External Consultants** (optional) | Bring specialized expertise or an independent audit perspective |
| **Physical Security** | Addresses building access, environmental controls, and physical threats |

### Why Cross-Functional?
- Security affects **every** department : it cannot be siloed in IT alone
- Business managers understand which assets are **most critical to operations**
- Legal and HR bring compliance and policy perspectives IT may miss
- End-user buy-in is essential : plans that ignore usability get circumvented

## 🤝 Assuring Commitment to a Security Plan

> **The biggest challenge isn't writing the plan : it's getting people to follow it.**

### Top-Down Commitment
- **Executive sponsorship is essential** : if leadership doesn't visibly support the plan, no one else will
- Security must be framed as a **business issue**, not just a technical one
- Budget allocation signals genuine commitment (not just lip service)
- Leadership should model good security behavior

### Building Organization-Wide Buy-In
- **Communication** : explain the "why" behind policies, not just the rules
- **Training** : regular, engaging security awareness programs (not just annual checkbox exercises)
- **Incentives** : recognize and reward good security practices
- **Consequences** : enforce policies consistently; non-compliance must have repercussions
- **Usability** : if security measures are too burdensome, people will work around them

### Ongoing Assurance Mechanisms
- **Regular audits** : internal and external reviews to verify adherence
- **Metrics and reporting** : track incidents, compliance rates, training completion
- **Tabletop exercises** : simulate incidents to test whether people know what to do
- **Continuous improvement** : update the plan based on lessons learned, new threats, and organizational changes
- **Contractual obligations** : include security responsibilities in employment contracts and vendor agreements

### Common Pitfalls
- ❌ Writing a plan that sits on a shelf and is never read
- ❌ Making it too technical for non-IT staff to understand
- ❌ Failing to update the plan after organizational or technological changes
- ❌ No accountability : everyone assumes someone else is responsible
- ❌ Over-reliance on technology without addressing human factors

---

## 💡 Key Takeaways

> [!tip] Remember
> - A security plan is a **living document**, not a one-time project
> - It must have **executive sponsorship** to succeed
> - The planning team must be **cross-functional**
> - The plan must balance **security with usability**
> - Regular **review, testing, and updating** are essential
