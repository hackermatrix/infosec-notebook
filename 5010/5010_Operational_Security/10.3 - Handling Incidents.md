# 10.3 : Handling Incidents

> **Core Idea:** Security incidents are inevitable. What matters is how quickly and effectively an organization **detects, responds to, contains, and recovers** from them. A well-prepared incident response capability minimizes damage and reduces recovery time and costs.

---

## 🔍 What is a Security Incident?

A **security incident** is any event that actually or potentially compromises the **confidentiality, integrity, or availability** of information or systems.

### Examples of Security Incidents
- Unauthorized access to systems or data
- Malware infection (ransomware, virus, worm, trojan)
- Denial of Service (DoS/DDoS) attack
- Data breach or data exfiltration
- Insider threat : employee stealing or leaking data
- Phishing attack that compromises credentials
- Physical theft of equipment (laptop, server, USB drive)
- Website defacement
- Privilege escalation
- Policy violation (e.g., sharing passwords, connecting unauthorized devices)

### Event vs. Incident
| Term | Definition |
|------|-----------|
| **Event** | Any observable occurrence in a system or network (not necessarily harmful) |
| **Incident** | An event that **violates security policy** or threatens security : requires a response |

> Not every event is an incident. A failed login attempt is an event. Fifty failed login attempts in 2 minutes is an incident.

---

## 📋 Incident Response Plans (IRP)

An **Incident Response Plan** is a documented, structured approach for handling and managing security incidents.

### Why You Need an IRP
- **Speed** : predefined procedures eliminate confusion during a crisis
- **Consistency** : ensures every incident gets a standardized, professional response
- **Legal protection** : demonstrates due diligence; preserves evidence for prosecution
- **Damage minimization** : faster containment = less harm
- **Regulatory compliance** : many regulations (GDPR, HIPAA, PCI-DSS) require documented incident response procedures
- **Learning** : structured post-incident analysis drives continuous improvement

### The Incident Response Lifecycle

The widely adopted **NIST SP 800-61** framework defines four phases:
####                 **Please Detect Cyber Events Properly**

#### Phase 1: Preparation
- Develop and document the IRP
- Build and train the Incident Response Team (see below)
- Deploy monitoring and detection tools (SIEM, IDS/IPS, antivirus, log management)
- Establish communication channels (internal and external)
- Create **incident classification scheme** (severity levels)
- Prepare an **incident response toolkit** (forensic tools, clean media, contact lists)
- Conduct regular drills and tabletop exercises

##### Incident Classification (Example)
| Severity | Description | Example | Response Time |
|----------|-------------|---------|--------------|
| **Critical (P1)** | Imminent threat to critical systems or data | Active ransomware attack, major data breach | Immediate (< 15 min) |
| **High (P2)** | Significant impact but contained or limited scope | Compromised admin account, malware on server | < 1 hour |
| **Medium (P3)** | Moderate impact, limited risk of spread | Single workstation infection, phishing attempt | < 4 hours |
| **Low (P4)** | Minimal impact, policy violation | Unauthorized software install, single failed login | < 24 hours |

#### Phase 2: Detection and Analysis
- **Detection sources:** IDS/IPS alerts, SIEM correlation, antivirus alerts, user reports, log analysis, threat intelligence feeds
- **Triage** : is this a real incident or a false positive?
- **Analysis** : determine scope, impact, attack vector, affected systems
- **Documentation** : begin recording every action taken (who, what, when, where)
- **Notification** : alert appropriate stakeholders based on severity

##### Indicators of Compromise (IoC)
- Unusual network traffic patterns or volume
- Unexpected system reboots or crashes
- Unauthorized user accounts or privilege changes
- Modified or missing log files
- Unusual outbound connections to unknown IP addresses
- File integrity changes on critical systems
- Reports from external parties (customers, law enforcement, CERT)

#### Phase 3: Containment, Eradication, and Recovery

##### Containment
- **Short-term containment** : immediate actions to stop the bleeding (isolate affected systems, block malicious IPs, disable compromised accounts)
- **Long-term containment** : more sustainable measures while preparing for eradication (apply patches, rebuild affected segments, implement additional monitoring)
- **Evidence preservation** : take forensic images before making changes; maintain chain of custody

> ⚠️ **Critical:** Contain first, then investigate. If you investigate without containing, the attacker may still be active and the damage continues.

##### Eradication
- **Remove the root cause** : delete malware, close exploited vulnerabilities, revoke compromised credentials
- **Identify all affected systems** : the visible compromise may be only part of the attack
- **Patch and harden** : fix the vulnerability that allowed the breach
- **Verify eradication** : scan, monitor, and confirm the threat is gone

##### Recovery
- **Restore from clean backups** : rebuild systems to a known-good state
- **Validate system integrity** : verify that restored systems are functioning correctly and securely
- **Monitor closely** : increased vigilance for signs of re-compromise
- **Gradual restoration** : bring systems back online in phases, starting with the most critical
- **User communication** : notify affected users and provide guidance

#### Phase 4: Post-Incident Activity (Lessons Learned)
- **Post-mortem / After-Action Review** : structured review of what happened, what worked, what didn't
- **Timeline reconstruction** : build a detailed chronological record
- **Root cause analysis** : why did the incident happen? What allowed it?
- **Improvement recommendations** : specific, actionable changes to prevent recurrence
- **Update the IRP** : incorporate lessons learned
- **Report** : document everything for management, legal, and regulatory purposes
- **Metrics** : track incident metrics over time (time to detect, time to contain, cost, recurrence)

##### Key Questions for the Post-Mortem
- What happened, and when?
- How was it detected? Could it have been detected sooner?
- Was the IRP followed? Where did it break down?
- What was the root cause?
- What was the total impact (financial, operational, reputational)?
- What specific improvements should be made?

## 🛡️ Incident Response Teams

### What is a CSIRT / IRT?

A **Computer Security Incident Response Team (CSIRT)**, also called an **Incident Response Team (IRT)**, is a group of skilled individuals responsible for managing security incidents.

### Team Composition

| Role | Responsibility |
|------|---------------|
| **Incident Response Manager / Lead** | Coordinates the response; makes decisions; communicates with management |
| **Security Analysts / Investigators** | Perform technical analysis, forensics, and threat hunting |
| **IT Operations / System Admins** | Execute containment and recovery actions on affected systems |
| **Network Engineers** | Analyze network traffic; implement network-level containment |
| **Forensic Specialists** | Collect and preserve digital evidence; maintain chain of custody |
| **Legal Counsel** | Advise on legal obligations, evidence handling, liability, and regulatory notification |
| **Communications / PR** | Handle external messaging : media, customers, partners |
| **HR Representative** | Involved when insiders are suspected or personnel actions are needed |
| **Management Representative** | Provides authority for decisions (shutting down systems, spending, etc.) |

### Team Models

| Model | Description | Best For |
|-------|-------------|----------|
| **Central** | Single team handles all incidents across the organization | Small to mid-size organizations |
| **Distributed** | Multiple teams for different locations or business units, coordinated centrally | Large or geographically dispersed organizations |
| **Hybrid** | Central coordination with specialized teams for specific domains (cloud, OT, etc.) | Complex enterprises |
| **Outsourced** | Third-party MSSP or IR firm provides incident response capability | Organizations lacking internal expertise |

### Team Readiness
- **Training** : regular skill development (forensics, malware analysis, threat intelligence)
- **Certifications** : GIAC (GCIH, GCFE), CISSP, CEH, CompTIA CySA+
- **Exercises** : tabletop exercises, capture-the-flag, red team vs. blue team simulations
- **Tools** : forensic workstations, network analyzers, malware sandboxes, SIEM access
- **Authority** : the team must have pre-authorized authority to take actions (isolate systems, disable accounts) without waiting for lengthy approval chains during a crisis
- **On-call rotation** : 24/7 coverage for critical environments

---

## 💡 Key Takeaways

> [!tip] Remember
> - The four phases: **Preparation → Detection & Analysis → Containment/Eradication/Recovery → Lessons Learned**
> - **Preparation** is the most important phase : it determines how well you handle everything else
> - **Contain first**, then investigate : stop the bleeding before doing surgery
> - **Document everything** : for legal, regulatory, and improvement purposes
> - An **untested plan** with an **unprepared team** is essentially no plan at all
> - **Post-incident review** is essential : every incident is a learning opportunity
