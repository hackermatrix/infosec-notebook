# 10.2 : Business Continuity Planning

> **Core Idea:** Business Continuity Planning (BCP) ensures that an organization can continue its **critical operations** during and after a disruption : whether it's a natural disaster, cyberattack, pandemic, or infrastructure failure. BCP goes beyond IT disaster recovery; it encompasses the entire business.

---

## 🔑 What is BCP?

**Business Continuity Planning (BCP)** is the process of creating a framework that helps an organization **prevent, prepare for, respond to, and recover from** disruptions that threaten its essential functions.

### BCP vs. Disaster Recovery (DR)
| Aspect | BCP | DR |
|--------|-----|-----|
| **Scope** | Entire organization : people, processes, technology | Primarily IT systems and data |
| **Focus** | Maintaining business operations | Restoring IT infrastructure |
| **Timeframe** | Before, during, and after a disruption | After a disruption |
| **Perspective** | Business-driven | Technology-driven |

> DR is a **subset** of BCP. You can have DR without BCP, but good BCP always includes DR.

---

## 📊 Step 1: Assess Business Impact (BIA)

The **Business Impact Analysis (BIA)** is the foundation of BCP. It identifies which functions are critical and what happens if they are disrupted.

### What the BIA Determines

#### Identify Critical Business Functions
- Which processes are essential to the organization's survival?
- Examples: payroll processing, customer-facing services, supply chain management, order fulfillment
- Not all functions are equally critical : the BIA **ranks** them

#### Determine Impact of Disruption
- **Financial impact** : lost revenue, penalties, legal costs
- **Operational impact** : inability to deliver products/services
- **Reputational impact** : loss of customer trust and brand damage
- **Legal/regulatory impact** : non-compliance fines, contractual breaches

#### Define Recovery Objectives
| Metric | Definition | Example |
|--------|-----------|---------|
| **RTO** (Recovery Time Objective) | Maximum acceptable downtime before function must be restored | "Email must be restored within 4 hours" |
| **RPO** (Recovery Point Objective) | Maximum acceptable data loss measured in time | "We can tolerate losing no more than 1 hour of transaction data" |
| **MTPD** (Maximum Tolerable Period of Disruption) | Absolute limit before damage becomes irreversible | "If the factory is down for more than 72 hours, we lose key contracts" |

#### Identify Dependencies
- What resources does each critical function need? (people, systems, data, facilities, vendors)
- What are the **upstream** and **downstream** dependencies?
- Single points of failure?

### BIA Process
1. **Identify** all business functions and processes
2. **Classify** them by criticality (Mission-Critical → Important → Non-Essential)
3. **Quantify** the impact of disruption over time (1 hour, 4 hours, 1 day, 1 week)
4. **Determine** RTOs and RPOs for each critical function
5. **Map** dependencies and single points of failure
6. **Document** findings and get management sign-off

---

## 🧠 Step 2: Develop Strategy

Based on the BIA, the organization develops **strategies** to maintain or quickly restore critical functions.

### Strategy Categories

#### People Strategies
- **Cross-training** : ensure multiple people can perform critical roles
- **Succession planning** : identify backups for key personnel
- **Remote work capabilities** : can staff work from home or alternate locations?
- **Communication plans** : how to reach staff during a disruption (emergency contact lists, mass notification systems)

#### Process Strategies
- **Manual workarounds** : can critical processes be done manually if systems fail?
- **Alternate procedures** : simplified workflows for crisis mode
- **Priority ordering** : which processes get restored first?

#### Technology Strategies
- **Backup and recovery** : regular backups with tested restoration procedures
- **Redundancy** : duplicate systems, networks, and data centers
- **Cloud-based solutions** : geographic distribution of infrastructure
- **Hot site / Warm site / Cold site** : alternate processing facilities

| Site Type | Description | Recovery Time | Cost |
|-----------|-------------|---------------|------|
| **Hot Site** | Fully equipped, real-time data replication, ready to operate immediately | Minutes to hours | Very High |
| **Warm Site** | Partially equipped, recent data backups, requires some setup | Hours to 1-2 days | Moderate |
| **Cold Site** | Empty facility with power and connectivity; all equipment must be brought in | Days to weeks | Low |
| **Mobile Site** | Portable, self-contained units (trailers) with IT equipment | Hours to days | Moderate |

#### Facility Strategies
- **Alternate work locations** : satellite offices, co-working spaces, partner facilities
- **Geographic separation** : backup sites should be far enough away that the same disaster doesn't affect both
- **Reciprocal agreements** : mutual aid agreements with partner organizations

#### Supply Chain Strategies
- **Multiple vendors** : avoid single-supplier dependencies
- **Safety stock** : inventory buffers for critical materials
- **Vendor BCP requirements** : require key suppliers to have their own BCPs

---

## 📝 Step 3: Develop the Plan

The actual BCP document translates the BIA findings and chosen strategies into actionable procedures.

### Plan Structure

#### 1. Plan Overview and Scope
- Purpose, scope, and assumptions
- Activation criteria : what triggers the plan?
- Authority : who can activate it?

#### 2. Roles and Responsibilities
- **BCP Coordinator** : owns the plan, coordinates exercises
- **Crisis Management Team** : senior leaders who make strategic decisions during a disruption
- **Recovery Teams** : functional teams responsible for restoring specific areas (IT, facilities, communications)
- **Contact information** : emergency contact lists (with redundant communication methods)

#### 3. Notification and Activation Procedures
- **Escalation tree** : who contacts whom and in what order
- **Decision criteria** : how to determine severity and whether to activate the plan
- **Communication templates** : pre-drafted messages for employees, customers, media, regulators

#### 4. Recovery Procedures
- Step-by-step instructions for restoring each critical function
- Prioritized by RTO (most time-sensitive first)
- Includes both technical procedures and business workarounds

#### 5. Resource Requirements
- Equipment, supplies, and personnel needed for recovery
- Vendor contact information and contract details
- Access credentials and documentation (stored securely off-site)

#### 6. Testing and Maintenance

##### Types of BCP Tests
| Test Type | Description | Effort |
|-----------|-------------|--------|
| **Checklist Review** | Team members review the plan individually | Low |
| **Tabletop Exercise** | Walk through a scenario in a meeting; discuss responses | Low-Medium |
| **Simulation / Walkthrough** | Simulate a disruption; teams practice their roles without actually activating systems | Medium |
| **Parallel Test** | Activate backup systems alongside live systems to verify they work | High |
| **Full Interruption Test** | Shut down primary systems and operate entirely from backups | Very High (risky) |

##### Maintenance Schedule
- **Review** the plan at least annually
- **Update** after any significant organizational change (mergers, new systems, personnel changes)
- **Revise** after any actual incident or exercise that reveals gaps
- **Distribute** updated copies to all relevant personnel
- **Audit** regularly to ensure compliance

---

## 💡 Key Takeaways

> [!tip] Remember
> - BCP is **business-driven**, not just an IT exercise
> - The **BIA** is the foundation : you can't plan recovery without knowing what's critical
> - **RTO** and **RPO** are the most important recovery metrics
> - Plans must be **tested** regularly : an untested plan is just a document
> - **People and processes** matter as much as technology
> - The plan is a **living document** that requires ongoing maintenance
