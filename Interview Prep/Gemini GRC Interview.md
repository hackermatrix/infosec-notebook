Basics:
**Cryptocurrency** **is digital currency that runs on decentralized networks** called **blockchains**, rather than being controlled by a central bank. It’s designed to work without the control of a central bank or government,  it operates on a **peer-to-peer network where users can make transactions directly with one another, bypassing intermediaries**. This independence from traditional financial institutions allows Bitcoin to be both **innovative and accessible*.

Bitcoin and Ethereum are the most well-known examples. A **crypto platform** like Gemini is essentially an exchange a place where people can **buy, sell, trade, and store cryptocurrencies.** Think of it like a stock brokerage, but for crypto assets instead of traditional stocks.


Why GRC Matters MORE at Gemini / Crypto

- **Regulatory Landscape is Evolving** — Unlike traditional finance where rules are well-established, **crypto regulation is still being written (MiCA in EU, unclear US federal framework). GRC teams must stay ahead of shifting requirements.**
- **Irreversibility of Transactions** — Crypto transactions cannot be reversed. A single security failure or unauthorized access can result in permanent, unrecoverable loss of funds
- https://support.world.org/hc/en-us/articles/33257935433875-Why-are-crypto-transactions-irreversible#:~:text=Crypto%20transactions%20are%20permanent%20and,Sufficient%20Balance%20is%20Required
- **Custodial Risk — Gemini holds $18B+ in customer crypto assets. The security controls around custody (hot wallets, cold storage) require rigorous risk assessment.**
- **Public Company Obligations** — Since the Sep 2025 IPO, Gemini now has SEC reporting requirements, SOX compliance considerations, and heightened transparency expectations.
- **Third-Party/Vendor Risk** — Crypto platforms integrate with blockchain networks, payment processors, banking partners, and technology vendors — each introducing unique risks.
- **Reputation Sensitivity** — The Gemini Earn/Genesis incident (2022-23) showed how a third-party failure can cascade into regulatory action and customer harm. Vendor risk management is critical.
- **Multi-Jurisdictional Compliance** — Operating in 70+ countries means navigating different regulatory regimes simultaneously.
