**The problem with passwords and OTPs**

When you type a password or OTP, that secret travels across the internet as data. Even if it's encrypted in transit, the server still receives and checks your actual secret. If the server gets breached, or if someone phishes you into typing your password on a fake site, they now have your credential and can reuse it.

**How a YubiKey actually works (the core idea)**

When you first register your YubiKey with a website (say, Google), the YubiKey generates a unique key pair a private key that never leaves the device, and a public key that gets sent to Google. Google stores the public key. The private key is locked inside the YubiKey's secure chip and cannot be extracted, not even by you.

**The login flow (challenge-response)**

When you log in later, here's what happens step by step. Google says "prove you're the real owner" and sends a random challenge — just a random string of data. Your browser passes that challenge to the YubiKey. The YubiKey signs the challenge using its private key (this requires you to physically touch the key, proving a human is present). The signed response goes back to Google. Google uses the stored public key to verify the signature.

The critical thing: the private key itself never travels anywhere. Google only sees the signed response, which is unique to that one challenge. Even if someone intercepts it, they can't reuse it because the next login will have a completely different challenge.

**Why phishing doesn't work**

This is the part most people miss. The YubiKey doesn't just sign the challenge — it also checks the origin, meaning the actual domain of the website. If you land on `go0gle.com` (a fake site), the YubiKey sees the wrong domain and the signature won't work on the real Google. This happens automatically at the hardware level. With passwords and OTPs, _you_ have to notice the fake domain. With a YubiKey, the cryptography itself rejects it.

**The different protocols you mentioned**

The YubiKey supports several modes. FIDO2/WebAuthn is the modern standard, which is the challenge-response flow I just described and the strongest option. FIDO U2F is the older version of the same idea, used as a second factor alongside a password. OTP mode is where the YubiKey generates a one-time password when you touch it — this is simpler but less phishing-resistant than FIDO2. OpenPGP and smart card modes let the YubiKey store encryption and signing keys for email, SSH, and similar uses.

**How it actually works**

When you visit `google.com` and try to log in, your browser is the one that knows the exact domain you're on. The browser takes Google's challenge and bundles it together with the origin (the actual domain: `https://google.com`). This bundled package is what gets sent to the YubiKey. The YubiKey then signs the entire bundle — challenge plus origin — as one unit.

So when Google receives the signed response, it can verify: "this signature was made over a challenge that includes `google.com` as the origin." If you had been on `go0gle.com`, the browser would have honestly reported that fake domain, the YubiKey would have signed a bundle containing `go0gle.com`, and when the real Google checks it, the origin wouldn't match. The signature is valid, but it's for the wrong domain.

**Why this is so powerful**

The key insight is that the browser cannot lie about the origin. This is enforced by the WebAuthn protocol specification — the browser is required to report the true domain. A phishing site can't tell your browser "pretend you're google.com" because the browser determines the origin itself from the actual URL.

So to break this system, an attacker would need to compromise the browser itself, not just trick the user. That's a dramatically higher bar than fooling someone with a convincing-looking fake login page.

**To summarize the chain of trust**: the browser honestly reports the domain, it bundles that with the challenge, the YubiKey signs the whole thing cryptographically, and the server verifies both the signature and the origin. No single piece does it alone — it's the combination that makes phishing fail.

**Traditional OTP and SMS** — no, these do not use WebAuthn. They're completely separate, older protocols. The code gets texted to you or generated by an app like Google Authenticator, and you just type it into a form. No origin binding, no cryptography between your device and the browser.

**Duo is more interesting though.** Duo actually supports multiple modes, and this is where it gets important.

When Duo sends you a push notification and you just tap "approve" on your phone, that is _not_ WebAuthn. It's better than SMS because the code isn't manually typed, but it's still vulnerable to phishing in a similar way. An attacker can trigger the push through a fake site, and if you tap approve without thinking, they're in. This is actually how several major companies got breached — attackers just spammed Duo push notifications until someone tapped approve out of annoyance.

However, Duo also supports WebAuthn/FIDO2 as one of its authentication methods. So if you configure Duo to use a YubiKey or a platform authenticator like Touch ID, then you _are_ getting the origin-binding protection we talked about.

**So the key takeaway**

It's not about the product (Duo, Okta, whatever) — it's about which protocol is being used underneath. The same platform can offer both weak and strong options. Duo with push notifications is dramatically weaker than Duo with a FIDO2 key, even though both say "Duo" on the screen.

This is why many companies after high-profile breaches have moved to requiring FIDO2 keys specifically, rather than just requiring "MFA" generically. Not all second factors are equal.
